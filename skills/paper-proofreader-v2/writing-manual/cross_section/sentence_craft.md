# Cross-Section: Sentence-Level Craft

> **Theoretical basis:** Williams & Bizup (Style, 13th ed.), Gopen & Swan (1990), Sword (2012)

---

## Core Principle: Characters as Subjects, Actions as Verbs

Williams & Bizup's master principle: **readers experience a sentence as clear when its grammatical subjects are the characters in the story, and its verbs express the actions those characters perform.** When writers hide characters in prepositional phrases and convert actions into abstract nouns, sentences become difficult — not because readers lack intelligence, but because the text is violating the processing logic of English.

---

## 1. Nominalization: Tool and Trap

Nominalization converts verbs and adjectives into nouns. It is the single most common source of obscurity in scientific writing.

**Common nominalization patterns:**
- Verb → Noun: investigate → investigation, analyze → analysis, distribute → distribution
- Verb → Noun (with suffix): observe → observation, conclude → conclusion, assess → assessment
- Adjective → Noun: relevant → relevance, accurate → accuracy, significant → significance

### When nominalization harms

❌ "The *investigation* of SOC *distribution* following *application* of freeze-thaw *treatment* was *conducted* by the team."
Every content word is a nominalization. The verbs ("was conducted") carry no meaning. No agent is clear.

✅ "We investigated how SOC distributes itself after freeze-thaw treatment."

**Diagnostic:** If you find "conduct," "perform," "carry out," "undertake," or "achieve" paired with a nominalization, the nominalization has converted a meaningful verb into a weak noun. Restore the verb.

| Weak pattern | Restoration |
|---|---|
| conduct an investigation of | investigate |
| perform an analysis of | analyze |
| carry out a measurement of | measure |
| make an observation of | observe |
| provide an explanation for | explain |
| achieve a reduction in | reduce |

### When nominalization helps

Nominalization serves two legitimate functions in scientific writing:

1. **Backward cohesion:** A nominalization that summarizes the preceding sentence's action creates a Given-New link.
   > "We analyzed the aggregate structure of all samples. *This analysis* revealed three distinct stability classes."
   Here, "this analysis" is a cohesive device — it connects backward.

2. **Conceptual reference:** When the concept itself is the topic, not the action.
   > "The *distribution* of SOC with depth follows a characteristic exponential decline."
   "Distribution" here refers to a pattern, not an action — nominalization is appropriate.

**Rule:** Nominalize when the concept (not the action) is genuinely the topic, or when the nominalization creates backward cohesion. De-nominalize otherwise.

## 2. Subject-Verb Proximity

**Gopen & Swan's first sentence-level principle:** Follow a grammatical subject as soon as possible with its verb.

Information inserted between subject and verb is processed as an interruption. The longer the gap, the higher the processing cost.

❌ "The SOC concentrations in the freeze-thaw treatment, which were measured at five time points over three months using methods adapted from Jones et al. (2019), were significantly higher than controls."
Subject (concentrations) → verb (were) separated by 20 words.

✅ "SOC concentrations in the freeze-thaw treatment were significantly higher than controls; we measured them at five time points over three months following Jones et al. (2019)."

**When interruption is necessary:** Place the interrupting material before the subject, or after the verb. Do not insert it between them.

---

## 3. End-Weight Principle

**Long, complex, or new information should appear toward the end of a sentence; short, familiar information at the beginning.**

This combines with Given-New: the topic position should be short and familiar; the stress position should be the longest, most complex, most important element.

❌ "A 40% increase in SOC under elevated CO₂ was found across all sites in this study."
❌ The new finding (40% increase) appears first; "in this study" — weak, known information — ends the sentence.

✅ "Across all sites, SOC concentrations under elevated CO₂ were 40% higher than ambient controls."

## 4. Active vs. Passive Voice

Voice choice should be driven by **what belongs in the topic position** — not by a rule about active vs. passive.

| Situation | Voice | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Standard laboratory procedure | Passive | The procedure matters; the agent is obvious |
| Non-standard or consequential decision | Active | The decision-maker's agency is rhetorically relevant |
| Interpretation or argument | Active | The author's intellectual agency should be visible |
| When the agent is unknown or irrelevant | Passive | — |

**Never avoid first person purely for stylistic reasons.** Interpreting results, making arguments, and explaining decisions all belong in active first person: "We interpret," "We chose," "We argue."

## 5. Verb Tense: A Cross-Section Reference

| Content | Tense |
|---|---|
| Established scientific facts | Present simple ("CO₂ absorbs infrared radiation") |
| Prior published findings | Past simple ("Smith et al. found that") |
| Ongoing research patterns | Present perfect ("Studies have shown that") |
| This study's methods | Past simple ("We collected," "were analyzed") |
| This study's specific findings | Past simple ("concentrations were higher") |
| Interpretation of findings | Present simple ("this suggests," "these results indicate") |
| Figure/table reference | Present simple ("Figure 3 shows") |
| General implications | Present simple ("these findings imply") |

**Two tenses can coexist in one sentence when the logic requires it:**
> "Einstein postulated [past — specific historical action] that the speed of light is [present — established truth] constant."

## 6. Sentence Length and Complexity

Sentence length itself is not the problem. **Structural clarity** is the issue. Very long sentences often signal two or three ideas compressed into one. The test: if you need to read a sentence twice to understand its main assertion, it needs to be split.

**When to split a sentence:**
- The sentence has two distinct main verbs of equal rhetorical weight
- An embedded clause introduces a new character or action that deserves its own sentence
- The sentence length exceeds ~40 words
- The sentence has two "however" structures

**When to keep a complex sentence:**
- The complexity reflects a genuinely complex logical relationship
- The length reflects end-weight (the long element belongs at the end)
- Splitting would destroy the Given-New connection between the parts

---

## 7. Agent Diagnostic Questions

- Are the main characters of the sentence in the subject position?
- Are the main actions expressed as verbs — or hidden as nominalizations?
- Is there a gap between the grammatical subject and its verb?
- Is the most important information at or near the sentence end?
- Is voice (active/passive) chosen for rhetorical reasons or by default?
- Does the tense match the epistemological status of the claim?
