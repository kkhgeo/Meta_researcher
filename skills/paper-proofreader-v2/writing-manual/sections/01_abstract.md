# Section Manual: Abstract

> **Theoretical basis:** Swales & Feak (2004), Day & Gastel (2022), Hyland (2005)

---

## Rhetorical Function

The Abstract is the single most-read part of any paper. It must function as a **self-contained argument**, not a table of contents. Its job is to make the reader want to read the full paper — and to make the contribution legible to readers who will never open it.

Expert abstracts deliver a **miniature IMRaD arc** in 150–300 words (journal-dependent): Background → Gap/Problem → Approach → Key Findings → Significance/Implication.

---

## Move Structure

| Move | Function | Typical language |
|---|---|---|
| **M1: Background** | Establish territory and justify relevance | Present tense; broad → narrow |
| **M2: Gap / Problem** | Create the niche — what is unknown or unresolved | "However," "remains unclear," "little is known about" |
| **M3: Purpose/Approach** | State what this paper does | "Here we show," "We report," "This study investigates" |
| **M4: Findings** | The core results — 1–3 key findings only | Past tense for specific results |
| **M5: Implications** | Why these results matter beyond this study | Present tense; forward-looking |

**Critical:** All five moves must be present. The most common expert-level failure is an M3→M4 abstract that omits M2 (no gap established) and M5 (no implication stated), making the contribution legible but not persuasive.

---

## Agent Checklist

### Structure
- [ ] Are all five moves present?
- [ ] Does M2 create a genuine niche (not just state the topic)?
- [ ] Does M5 go beyond restating findings to stating significance?
- [ ] Is the abstract self-contained — no undefined abbreviations, no citation numbers?

### Content
- [ ] Does the abstract accurately reflect the paper's actual contribution?
- [ ] Are the 1–3 most important findings highlighted (not a comprehensive list)?
- [ ] Is the scope of the claim appropriate — neither under- nor overstated?

### Language
- [ ] Is M1–M2 in present tense, M4 in past tense, M5 in present tense?
- [ ] Is the abstract free of vague openers ("This study aims to...")?
- [ ] Does the abstract begin with content, not with "In this paper..."?

---

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Missing the niche (M2 absent)
❌ "Soil organic carbon plays a key role in nutrient cycling. We measured SOC across 50 sites and found..."
✅ "Soil organic carbon plays a key role in nutrient cycling. However, how SOC responds to repeated freeze-thaw cycles remains poorly quantified. We measured..."

### 2. Vague implication (M5 weak)
❌ "These results improve our understanding of SOC dynamics."
✅ "These results suggest that freeze-thaw intensity — not cumulative temperature — is the primary driver of SOC loss, with implications for carbon budget modeling under projected warming scenarios."

### 3. Methods overwhelm findings
The abstract is not a methods summary. If M3 takes more than one sentence, the abstract is likely inverted in emphasis.

### 4. Tense inconsistency
- Background, definitions, established facts → **present**
- What this paper did → **past** ("we collected") or **present** ("we show")
- Specific findings → **past** ("concentrations were 40% higher")
- Implications, general claims → **present**

---

## Verb Choices by Move

| Move | Strong verb examples |
|---|---|
| M3 (purpose) | report, demonstrate, show, characterize, quantify, evaluate, propose, develop |
| M4 (findings) | found, observed, detected, identified, revealed, showed, demonstrated |
| M5 (implications) | suggest, indicate, provide, enable, support, challenge, advance |

Avoid in M3: "aims to," "attempts to," "tries to" — these hedge the contribution before presenting it.
