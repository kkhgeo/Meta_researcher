# Section Manual: Results & Discussion (Combined)

> **Theoretical basis:** Brett (1994), Yang & Allison (2003), Hyland (2005)

---

## Rhetorical Function

The combined Results & Discussion format is common in high-impact journals (e.g., *Nature*, *PNAS*, *GCA*) when findings and their interpretation are deeply intertwined. The challenge is integrating two distinct rhetorical modes — **reporting** (Results logic) and **arguing** (Discussion logic) — without losing the reader or blurring the evidentiary basis of claims.

The core discipline: **each cycle must be anchored in data before it is interpreted**. Present → Interpret → Connect to literature → Advance, in that order for each major finding.

---

## The R&D Cycle

For each major finding, the combined section executes a four-part cycle:

| Step | Function | Tense |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Present** | State the finding with data | Past |
| **2. Interpret** | Explain what the finding means | Present |
| **3. Compare** | Connect to prior literature — agreement or contrast | Past/Present mix |
| **4. Advance** | State what this adds to knowledge | Present |

This cycle then leads directly into the next finding, creating forward momentum through the argument.

---

## Agent Checklist

### Cycle integrity
- [ ] Is each major finding presented with data before being interpreted?
- [ ] Is the interpretation clearly tied to the specific data just presented (not a general claim)?
- [ ] Does each interpretation cycle connect to relevant prior work?
- [ ] Does each cycle close with a statement of what the finding contributes?

### Transitions between cycles
- [ ] Are transitions between findings explicit and logical?
- [ ] Does the sequence of findings follow an argument — not just the order of data collection?
- [ ] Is the overall narrative coherent — does reading the section reveal a unified story?

### Boundary discipline
- [ ] Are there pure data statements ("we found X") without any analytical follow-through?
- [ ] Are there interpretive claims that appear before their supporting data?
- [ ] Are there speculative claims that exceed what the data support?

### Stance calibration
- [ ] Are hedges calibrated to evidence strength (not uniformly cautious or uniformly assertive)?
- [ ] Are comparisons to prior literature specific (named studies, specific claims) not vague ("previous work has shown")?
- [ ] Is the contribution language confident without being overstated?

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Presentation without interpretation
❌ "SOC concentrations increased by 40% in the freeze-thaw treatment (p < 0.01). This is shown in Figure 3."
✅ "SOC concentrations increased by 40% in the freeze-thaw treatment relative to controls (p < 0.01; Figure 3), suggesting that repeated freeze-thaw cycles accelerate aggregate disruption and expose previously protected carbon to microbial attack."

### 2. Interpretation without data anchor
❌ "These results demonstrate that freeze-thaw cycles are the primary driver of SOC loss in Arctic soils."
This is over-interpretation — the data support A being *a* driver, not *the primary* driver, unless the design explicitly tested all drivers.

### 3. Literature comparison too vague
❌ "This is consistent with previous studies."
✅ "This finding aligns with Mu et al. (2022), who reported similar increases in dissolved organic carbon following simulated freeze-thaw events in Tibetan Plateau soils, suggesting the mechanism may be broadly applicable across cryogenic ecosystems."

### 4. Buried contribution
The "Advance" step of each cycle is the most important sentence in the cycle. It should not appear as a subordinate clause. Give it a sentence of its own.

### 5. Losing the thread between cycles
Each cycle should end with a sentence that either completes the finding's significance OR explicitly sets up the next finding: "Having established X, we next examine how Y modulates this response."

## Tense Mixing Guide

| Purpose | Tense |
|---|---|
| Reporting this study's specific results | Past ("concentrations were higher") |
| Interpreting / stating implications | Present ("this suggests," "this indicates") |
| Referencing prior work | Past ("Smith et al. found") |
| Stating accepted general knowledge | Present ("freeze-thaw cycles disrupt soil aggregates") |
| Comparing with literature | Mixed: "Smith et al. (2021) found [past]... Our results extend this [present]..." |

---

## Stance Calibration for Each Step

| Step | Appropriate markers |
|---|---|
| Present (data) | Factual; minimal hedging |
| Interpret | "suggests," "indicates," "implies," "is consistent with" |
| Compare (agreement) | "aligns with," "is consistent with," "corroborates," "extends" |
| Compare (contrast) | "contrasts with," "differs from," "cannot be explained by" |
| Advance (strong) | "demonstrates," "establishes," "provides evidence that" |
| Advance (moderate) | "supports the hypothesis that," "is consistent with the view that" |
