# Cross-Section: Stance, Hedging & Academic Voice

> **Theoretical basis:** Hyland Metadiscourse Framework (2005, 2019), Swales & Feak (2004)

---

## Core Principle: Academic Writing Is Persuasion, Not Reporting

Scientific papers are social acts within disciplinary communities. Every sentence projects an epistemic position — a claim about how certain the writer is, how much they respect prior work, how they want the reader to evaluate the evidence. Hyland's metadiscourse framework maps the full system.

**Two systems work simultaneously in every text:**
- **Interactive resources** — guide the reader through the text
- **Interactional resources** — engage the reader in evaluating the argument

---

## 1. Interactive Metadiscourse

These resources guide the reader through the text structure without engaging their evaluation.

| Type | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Transitions** | Signal logical relations | however, therefore, consequently, although, while |
| **Frame markers** | Organize text structure | firstly, to conclude, in this section, turning to |
| **Endophoric markers** | Reference within the text | see Figure 3, as noted above, cf. Table 1 |
| **Evidentials** | Attribute claims to sources | according to X, as Y argued, Smith et al. reported |
| **Code glosses** | Clarify or elaborate | namely, in other words, that is, for example |

### Agent guidance
- **Transitions** should signal genuine logical relationships — not be used as generic connectors. "Furthermore" means additive; "however" means contrastive; "therefore" means consequential. Misuse undermines the argument's logic.
- **Evidentials** are a primary stance device: they allow writers to report others' claims without endorsing them. "X argues that..." is non-committal; "X demonstrates that..." endorses X's claim.
- **Code glosses** should be calibrated to audience expertise. Expert-to-expert writing rarely needs them; interdisciplinary writing uses them frequently.

---

## 2. Interactional Metadiscourse

These resources shape the reader's engagement with the argument.

### 2a. Hedges
Hedges reduce the writer's commitment to a proposition, inviting the reader to evaluate the evidence independently.

| Hedge type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Modal verbs** | may, might, could, would, should |
| **Lexical verbs** | suggest, indicate, appear, seem, tend to, imply |
| **Adverbs** | possibly, probably, perhaps, generally, often |
| **Adjectives** | possible, probable, likely, uncertain |
| **Approximators** | approximately, around, about, roughly |
| **Conditional clauses** | if these results hold, assuming X |

**Critical principle: hedge strength must match evidence strength.**

| Evidence quality | Appropriate hedge |
|---|---|
| Directly measured, statistically robust | No hedge, or "demonstrates," "shows" |
| Well-supported but not directly demonstrated | "suggests," "indicates," "is consistent with" |
| Plausible inference from indirect evidence | "may," "might," "could" |
| Speculation beyond current data | "it is possible that," "one might speculate" |

### 2b. Boosters
Boosters strengthen commitment to a proposition.

| Booster examples | Usage guidance |
|---|---|
| clearly, obviously, certainly, undoubtedly | Require exceptional evidential support — reviewers will scrutinize |
| demonstrate, establish, prove, confirm | Strong claims requiring strong data |
| always, never, all, every | Risky universals — quantify if possible |
| in fact, indeed, it is clear that | Reader should be able to independently verify |

**Warning:** Overuse of boosters in Discussion sections is a common error that erodes credibility. Reserve strong boosters for claims that are genuinely well-established.

### 2c. Attitude markers
Express the writer's evaluation of the information.

| Examples | Use with care |
|---|---|
| importantly, significantly, surprisingly, remarkably | Should reflect genuine significance — not deployed routinely |
| unfortunately, ideally, notably | Occasional use acceptable; frequent use reads as editorializing |

### 2d. Self-mention
First-person pronouns signal agency, responsibility, and confidence.

**Principle:** Use first person for decisions, interpretations, and arguments. Use passive for standard procedures.

| Active (first person) | Passive |
|---|---|
| "We chose ICP-MS because..." | "Samples were analyzed by ICP-MS" |
| "We interpret this pattern as..." | — |
| "Our findings suggest..." | — |

Avoiding first person entirely creates what Sword calls "zombie prose" — text where no one takes responsibility for intellectual claims.

### 2e. Engagement markers
Address the reader directly to involve them in the argument.

| Examples | Effect |
|---|---|
| "Note that..." | Directs attention |
| "Consider the implication..." | Invites reasoning |
| "As we will show..." | Creates anticipation |
| "Readers familiar with X will recognize..." | Builds shared ground |

## 3. Discipline-Specific Calibration

Hedging norms vary by discipline and journal. Before submitting, audit:
1. Read 5 published articles from the target journal
2. Note the hedge-to-booster ratio in Discussion sections
3. Compare with your manuscript

**Known patterns across STEM fields:**
- Hard physical sciences (chemistry, physics): higher booster frequency, stronger claims
- Environmental/ecological sciences: moderate hedging, emphasis on conditional claims
- Social and behavioral sciences: highest hedge frequency

---

## 4. Common Expert-Level Stance Errors

### Over-hedging (contribution collapse)
❌ "These results may possibly suggest that freeze-thaw cycling could potentially play some role in SOC dynamics."
Three hedges on one claim is not caution — it is evasion. The reader cannot evaluate the contribution.

### Under-hedging (overreach)
❌ "These results demonstrate that freeze-thaw frequency controls SOC loss in all Arctic soil types."
"Demonstrate," "controls," and "all" are individually strong. Together, they make a claim the study cannot support.

### Hedge mismatch
❌ "This clearly suggests..." — "clearly" (booster) and "suggests" (hedge) contradict each other. Choose one epistemic position.

### Uniformly passive evidentials
❌ Using "it has been shown that" for every citation obscures whether the writer endorses or merely acknowledges the cited claim. Vary: "X found," "X argued," "X proposes," "X demonstrates."

---

## 5. Agent Diagnostic Questions

- Is each hedged claim using the appropriate hedge for its evidence strength?
- Are any boosters present that the data cannot fully support?
- Are self-mentions absent where the author's interpretive agency should be claimed?
- Is "however" used for genuine contrast — or as a generic paragraph opener?
- Are evidentials varied — or is every citation introduced with "it has been shown that"?
