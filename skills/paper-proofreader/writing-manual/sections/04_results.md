# Section Manual: Results

> **Theoretical basis:** Brett (1994), Leong et al. (2018), Gopen & Swan (1990)

---

## Rhetorical Function

Results present the data. But expert Results sections do not merely narrate numbers — they **guide the reader's attention** toward the most important patterns, in the most logical order, using text and visuals as complementary tools. The Results section is not a transcript of what happened during data collection; it is a curated argument about what the data show.

The key discipline: **report the pattern; reserve interpretation for Discussion**. The boundary is permeable in practice, but clear in principle — Results statements are data-grounded observations; Discussion statements are inferences about what those observations mean.

---

## Move Structure (Brett 1994)

Results sections cycle through three functional move types:

| Move type | Function | Signal phrases |
|---|---|---|
| **Metatextual (M)** | Prepare the reader for upcoming information | "Figure 1 shows...", "As illustrated in Table 2..." |
| **Presentation (P)** | Report the finding | "Concentrations increased by 40%...", "We observed..." |
| **Comment (C)** | Note significance or relationship within the data | "This pattern was most pronounced in...", "Notably..." |

The cycle is **M → P → C**, repeated for each finding. Expert Results sections have high thematic density — each cycle is compact and the transitions between cycles are explicit.

## Agent Checklist

### Structure and sequencing
- [ ] Are findings presented in order of importance, or in a logical analytical order — not the order data happened to be collected?
- [ ] Does each paragraph address one finding or one coherent group of findings?
- [ ] Are metatextual moves present to orient the reader before each finding?
- [ ] Is each finding followed by a comment identifying its significance within the data?

### Text-figure relationship
- [ ] Is the text complementary to (not redundant with) the figures and tables?
- [ ] Does the text highlight the key pattern, while the figure/table provides detail?
- [ ] Are all figures and tables referenced in the text in order?
- [ ] Is figure/table reference in present tense ("Figure 3 shows") while findings are in past tense?

### Interpretation boundary
- [ ] Are there interpretive statements that belong in Discussion?
- [ ] Are causal claims present that are not directly supported by the presented data?

### Statistical reporting
- [ ] Are effect sizes reported alongside significance values?
- [ ] Are uncertainty measures reported (SD, SE, CI, range) consistently?
- [ ] Are comparisons qualified by the relevant grouping variable?

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Narrating data instead of reporting patterns
❌ "In Group A, the mean was 42.3 ± 3.1. In Group B, the mean was 51.7 ± 4.2. In Group C, the mean was 38.9 ± 2.8."
✅ "Group B showed significantly higher values than both Group A and Group C (51.7 ± 4.2 vs. 42.3 ± 3.1 and 38.9 ± 2.8, respectively; p < 0.01), suggesting a non-linear response to treatment intensity."

### 2. Interpretation drift into Discussion territory
❌ "The 40% increase in SOC under elevated CO₂ indicates that carbon sequestration in these soils is primarily driven by root exudate inputs rather than reduced decomposition — a finding that challenges current model parameterization."
The second clause is Discussion, not Results. Results should stop at the observation.

### 3. Text that duplicates figures
If the text reads "As shown in Figure 2, concentrations in Site A were higher than in Site B, C, and D across all time points," and Figure 2 shows exactly this — one of them is redundant. The text should say *why this pattern matters*, not repeat what the figure already shows.

### 4. Missing statistical context
Results without uncertainty measures ("SOC increased") are uninterpretable. Every quantitative claim needs an uncertainty range.

### 5. Passive overuse obscuring the agent of observation
"An increase was observed" is weaker than "We observed an increase" when authorial observation is the relevant action. Use passive when the procedure matters; active when the observer's judgment matters.

## Tense Conventions

| Content | Tense |
|---|---|
| Specific findings from this study | Past simple ("concentrations were higher") |
| Reference to figures/tables | Present simple ("Figure 2 shows") |
| Statistical values, reported facts | Past simple |
| Patterns that hold generally | Present simple (with care — this approaches interpretation) |

---

## Information Order Within Sentences

Follow the Given-New principle strictly in Results:
- **Begin sentences** with a reference to the variable, condition, or comparison just established
- **End sentences** with the numerical finding or observed pattern

❌ "A 40% increase under elevated CO₂ was found for SOC concentrations."
✅ "SOC concentrations under elevated CO₂ were 40% higher than in ambient controls."
