# Section Manual: Conclusion

> **Theoretical basis:** Day & Gastel (2022), Swales & Feak (2004), Hyland (2005)

---

## Rhetorical Function

The Conclusion answers the question: **"So what?"** It does not repeat the Discussion. It distills the study's contribution into a final, memorable argument — what the field now knows that it did not before, and what that knowledge makes possible.

The Conclusion is the last thing a reader reads and the section most likely to be read by a reader who skimmed everything else. It must stand as a coherent, persuasive endpoint on its own.

---

## Summary vs. Conclusion: A Critical Distinction

| Summary | Conclusion |
|---|---|
| Repeats what was done and found | States what has been established |
| Describes the study | Argues for the contribution |
| Past-focused | Forward-focused |
| "We measured X and found Y" | "These results establish that Z, with implications for A and B" |

**Expert Conclusions are argumentative, not descriptive.** A Conclusion that reads like a compressed Abstract has failed its rhetorical function.

---

## Move Structure

| Move | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **M1: Restate principal findings** | Consolidate the 1–3 most important results | Distill, don't repeat. One sentence per finding maximum. |
| **M2: State the contribution** | What does the field now know? | The most important sentence in the paper. |
| **M3: State implications** | What does this knowledge enable or suggest? | Specific > vague. |
| **M4: Acknowledge limitations** | Brief scope qualification | Optional; if present, should be one sentence only. |
| **M5: Future directions** | What questions remain? | Optional; must be specific to be useful. |

---

## Agent Checklist

### Content
- [ ] Is M2 (contribution statement) present and clearly articulated?
- [ ] Are findings *distilled* rather than repeated from Results/Discussion?
- [ ] Is there any new data or new claims appearing for the first time in the Conclusion?
- [ ] Are implications specific enough to be actionable?

### Tone and stance
- [ ] Is the contribution stated confidently — not qualified into invisibility?
- [ ] Does the Conclusion avoid false modesty ("This study provides a first step toward understanding...")?
- [ ] Are future directions specific research questions, not generic calls for "more research"?

### Boundaries
- [ ] Does the Conclusion avoid introducing new evidence, new citations, or new arguments?
- [ ] Is the limitation statement (if present) brief and framed as a scope qualifier?
- [ ] Is the section appropriately concise — no padding or throat-clearing?

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Conclusion that is a compressed Abstract
Repeating the background, methods, results, and implications in miniature is a Summary, not a Conclusion. The reader already read the paper. The Conclusion should feel like elevation to a higher vantage point — not a replay.

### 2. Missing contribution statement
❌ "In this study, we collected SOC samples from 50 sites and measured freeze-thaw effects. We found significant increases in mineralization. Future work should examine microbial community responses."
✅ "Freeze-thaw frequency — independent of cumulative temperature — governs SOC mineralization rates in discontinuous permafrost zones, establishing a mechanistic basis for revising temperature-driven parameterizations in Earth system models."

### 3. Over-hedged contribution
❌ "These results may suggest that freeze-thaw cycling could potentially play a role in SOC dynamics."
This is the contribution hedged to meaninglessness. The Conclusion is where you claim your results. Match the hedge to the evidence, but do not collapse the contribution.

### 4. Generic future directions
❌ "Future studies should examine SOC dynamics in other regions."
✅ "Future work should test whether the freeze-thaw frequency threshold identified here (>5 cycles per season) applies to organic-rich soils, where different aggregate stability mechanisms may alter the mineralization response."

### 5. New citations or data
Nothing new should appear in the Conclusion. Every claim here should have been established in the preceding sections.

## Tense Conventions

| Content | Tense |
|---|---|
| Referring to this study's findings | Past ("we found," "results showed") |
| Stating the contribution / what is now established | Present ("this study establishes," "these results demonstrate") |
| Implications and significance | Present ("these findings suggest," "this provides") |
| Future directions | Modal future ("future work should," "remains to be tested") |

---

## The Contribution Sentence

Every Conclusion should contain one sentence — usually early — that could serve as the paper's contribution statement in a grant proposal. It should be:

- **Specific** — tied to this study's actual findings, not the field generally
- **Positive** — states what is now known, not what was unknown before
- **Forward-pointing** — explains why this knowledge matters

A useful template: *"[Finding], which [establishes / revises / extends / challenges] [existing understanding], with implications for [specific application or theoretical question]."*
