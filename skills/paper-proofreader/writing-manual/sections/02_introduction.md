# Section Manual: Introduction

> **Theoretical basis:** Swales CARS model (1990, 2004), Doumont (Nature Scitable), McEnerney (UChicago)

---

## Rhetorical Function

The Introduction must do one thing above all others: **justify the existence of this paper within the disciplinary conversation**. It is not a literature review. It is a strategic argument for why the gap this paper addresses is real, significant, and worth filling — and why this paper fills it in a way no prior work has.

McEnerney's principle applies forcefully here: reviewers don't ask "Is this correct?" — they ask "Does reading this change what I know or believe?" The Introduction must demonstrate that the answer is yes.

---

## Move Structure: Swales CARS Model (2004 Revision)

### Move 1 — Establish a Territory
Signal that the topic is important and that prior work exists.

**Sub-moves (not all required):**
- Claim centrality: assert the topic's significance
- Make topic generalizations: characterize the state of knowledge
- Review prior research: survey existing work

**Expert execution:** Do not simply summarize the literature. Weave literature review throughout Move 1 to build a coherent picture of what is known — not a chronological list of who studied what.

### Move 2 — Establish a Niche
This is the critical move. Create a space for the present work by showing something is incomplete, inconsistent, or absent.

**Four strategies (non-exclusive):**
1. **Counter-claim:** "However, these studies assumed X, which overlooks Y..."
2. **Gap indication:** "Despite this progress, the mechanism by which X affects Y remains unclear."
3. **Question-raising:** "This raises the question of whether these patterns hold under Z conditions."
4. **Continuing a tradition:** "Building on this framework, we extend the analysis to..."

**Expert execution:** Avoid the blunt "No studies have examined..." formulation — it is often factually suspect and signals novice writing. Prefer nuanced contrastive framing: "Prior work has focused primarily on A, leaving the role of B largely unaddressed."

### Move 3 — Occupy the Niche
Announce the present work and its contribution.

**Sub-moves:**
- Announce the purpose/research ("In this study, we...")
- State the approach/methodology (briefly)
- List principal findings (optional; common in STEM)
- State the value of the contribution
- Preview the paper structure (optional; common in some disciplines)

## Agent Checklist

### Architecture
- [ ] Does the Introduction follow a funnel structure (broad → narrow → specific contribution)?
- [ ] Is Move 2 present and clearly articulated — is the gap specific and arguable?
- [ ] Does Move 3 follow logically from Move 2 (the study addresses exactly the gap that was established)?
- [ ] Does the Introduction end with a clear, confident statement of what this paper contributes?
- [ ] Are the three moves present even if not in strict linear order?

### Literature integration
- [ ] Is literature review woven into the argument rather than listed chronologically?
- [ ] Does each citation serve a rhetorical purpose (supporting a claim, establishing precedent, identifying a gap)?
- [ ] Are there any "orphan citations" — references that appear without clear rhetorical function?

### Scope and tone
- [ ] Is the gap claim falsifiable and specific — not "little is known about X" when substantial work exists?
- [ ] Is the contribution stated confidently — not hedged to the point of invisibility?
- [ ] Does the Introduction avoid announcing findings in detail (that belongs in Results)?

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Move 2 is vague
❌ "The mechanisms of SOC dynamics are not fully understood."
✅ "Existing models attribute SOC loss primarily to microbial respiration, yet the contribution of freeze-thaw cycling to carbon mineralization remains unresolved — a gap that limits model accuracy in high-latitude systems."

### 2. Move 3 does not answer Move 2
If Move 2 establishes a gap in mechanistic understanding, Move 3 must describe a study that addresses that mechanism — not just any study on the topic.

### 3. Contribution buried or absent
The contribution statement should appear in the final 1–2 sentences of the Introduction, stated clearly and without false modesty. If it requires searching for, it needs to be rewritten.

### 4. Literature review as chronological summary
The Introduction is not the place to demonstrate reading breadth. Every citation should advance the argument toward the gap.

### 5. Over-broad opening
Starting with planetary-scale claims ("Climate change poses an existential threat...") before narrowing creates throat-clearing, not context. Start one level above the actual topic.

## Tense Conventions

| Content | Tense |
|---|---|
| Established facts and accepted knowledge | Present simple |
| Prior studies and their findings | Past simple ("Smith et al. (2020) found...") |
| Ongoing research trends | Present perfect ("Studies have shown...") |
| The present study's purpose | Present simple or past ("This study investigates / investigated") |
| Paper preview ("Section 2 describes...") | Present simple |

---

## Useful Contrastive Structures for Move 2

- "While X has been well characterized, Y remains..."
- "Prior work has established A; however, the relationship between A and B is less clear."
- "Despite considerable progress in X, it is unclear whether..."
- "These findings raise the question of whether..."
- "Most studies have examined X under Y conditions, leaving Z unaddressed."
