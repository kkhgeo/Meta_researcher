# Section Manual: Discussion

> **Theoretical basis:** Yang & Allison (2003), Peacock (2002), McEnerney, Hyland (2005)

---

## Rhetorical Function

The Discussion is the most difficult and most consequential section. Its function is not to summarize Results — it is to **argue for the meaning of those results within the disciplinary community**. McEnerney's formulation is the clearest: the Discussion should not express what you think; it should change what the reader thinks.

The Discussion is where the paper's intellectual contribution lives. Weak discussions produce papers that get cited for data but not for ideas. Strong discussions position the work within ongoing theoretical and empirical conversations, articulate what the field now knows that it didn't before, and identify the most generative remaining questions.

---

## Move Structure (Yang & Allison 2003)

| Move | Obligatory? | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **M1: Background information** | Optional | Re-orient reader to the research context |
| **M2: Reporting results** | Obligatory | Briefly restate key findings (not repeat — distill) |
| **M3: Interpreting results** | Obligatory | Explain the meaning of the findings |
| **M4: Comparing with literature** | Obligatory | Position findings relative to prior work |
| **M5: Explaining unexpected results** | Optional | Address discrepancies or anomalies |
| **M6: Stating limitations** | Optional | Acknowledge scope constraints honestly |
| **M7: Making recommendations** | Optional | Suggest applications or future directions |

**Note:** In practice, M2, M3, and M4 are deeply intertwined and cycle together, not sequentially.

---

## Agent Checklist

### Opening
- [ ] Does the Discussion open with the key finding — not a restatement of the purpose?
- [ ] Is the opening sentence interpretive, not merely descriptive?
- [ ] Is the most important result foregrounded?

### Interpretation quality
- [ ] Are interpretations explicitly tied to specific data?
- [ ] Are causal claims supported by the study design (correlational data cannot support causal claims without further argument)?
- [ ] Is the interpretive logic spelled out — not left implicit?

### Literature engagement
- [ ] Is comparison with prior work specific (named studies, specific claims)?
- [ ] Are agreements, contrasts, and extensions all addressed where relevant?
- [ ] Are unexpected or contradictory findings explained — not ignored?

### Limitations (M6)
- [ ] Are limitations present?
- [ ] Are limitations framed as scope qualifiers, not fatal flaws?
- [ ] Are limitations positioned after the main interpretive argument — not before?

### Implications (M7)
- [ ] Is there a hierarchy: methodological implications → disciplinary implications → broader implications?
- [ ] Are implications concrete enough to be useful — not vague ("this improves understanding of X")?

### Closing
- [ ] Does the Discussion end with the most important implication or the most generative remaining question?
- [ ] Does the closing statement justify why this paper was worth reading?

## Common Expert-Level Problems

### 1. Discussion opens by restating the purpose
❌ "This study aimed to investigate the effects of freeze-thaw cycling on SOC dynamics in Arctic soils."
✅ "Repeated freeze-thaw cycles consistently increased SOC mineralization rates across all study sites, with the magnitude of effect scaling with freeze-thaw frequency rather than cumulative temperature exposure."

### 2. Results repeated, not interpreted
❌ "SOC concentrations were 40% higher in the freeze-thaw treatment."
✅ "The 40% increase in SOC under freeze-thaw cycling is most parsimoniously explained by aggregate disruption releasing physically protected carbon, consistent with the micro-structural analysis of [cite]."

### 3. Literature comparison vague or absent
❌ "This is consistent with previous work."
✅ "This finding aligns with Mu et al. (2022) and contrasts with Liu et al. (2020), who reported no significant effect — a discrepancy likely attributable to differences in soil texture (clay-rich vs. sandy) affecting aggregate stability."

### 4. Limitations that undermine rather than qualify
❌ "A limitation of this study is that our results may not be applicable to non-Arctic soils, our sample size was small, and we did not measure microbial community composition."
Each limitation needs to be framed within what the study *can* conclude. Listing multiple limitations without qualification signals defensiveness, not rigor.

✅ "Our findings are most directly applicable to mineral soils in discontinuous permafrost zones; whether the same mechanism operates in organic-rich soils warrants separate investigation."

### 5. Implications without specificity
❌ "These results have implications for carbon cycle modeling."
✅ "These results suggest that current Earth system models, which parameterize freeze-thaw effects primarily as a function of mean annual temperature, may systematically underestimate SOC loss in regions with high freeze-thaw frequency under projected warming."

## Tense Conventions

| Content | Tense |
|---|---|
| Referring back to this study's results | Past ("we found," "concentrations were") |
| Interpreting results | Present ("this suggests," "this indicates") |
| Referring to prior studies | Past ("Smith et al. found") |
| Stating accepted scientific knowledge | Present ("freeze-thaw cycles disrupt aggregates") |
| Stating implications | Present ("these results suggest that models should...") |

---

## Claim-Strength Calibration

The Discussion must calibrate claim strength to evidence quality. Use this hierarchy:

| Evidence strength | Appropriate language |
|---|---|
| Directly demonstrated by this study | "demonstrate," "show," "establish" |
| Well-supported but not directly proven | "suggest," "indicate," "support the hypothesis that" |
| Plausible but speculative | "may," "might," "could," "it is possible that" |
| Acknowledged alternative interpretation | "alternatively," "one possibility is that" |

**Never use "prove" in empirical science writing.** Never use "clearly" or "obviously" without strong data support — these boosters invite reviewer skepticism.
