# Cross-Section: Advanced Non-Native Writer Issues

> **Theoretical basis:** Nesselhauf (2005), Hyland (2019), Hasselgren (1994), corpus-based L2 writing research

---

## Framing Principle

At expert levels, writing quality is primarily an **expertise problem, not a language problem**. Expert non-native writers converge toward expert native-speaker norms across most dimensions. The issues addressed here are the **residual gaps** that persist even at high proficiency — subtle patterns that signal to reviewers and editors that something is slightly off, without triggering easily identifiable grammar errors.

Do not flag these issues unless they create genuine ambiguity or credibility problems. The goal is not to eliminate all L1 influence; it is to identify patterns that undermine the paper's persuasive force.

---

## 1. Collocational Errors

**The most impactful subtle issue.** Nesselhauf (2005) found that approximately one-third of advanced L2 writers' collocations remain non-standard, with L1 influence accounting for nearly half of errors.

Collocational errors create **lexical dissonance** — the reader senses something is wrong without being able to identify it, increasing processing burden and subtly undermining credibility.

### Common academic verb-noun collocation errors

| Non-standard | Standard |
|---|---|
| do a conclusion | draw a conclusion |
| make an experiment | conduct / run an experiment |
| do research on | conduct research on / research (verb) |
| make income | earn income |
| high possibility | strong possibility / high probability |
| solve a problem | address a problem (for research gaps) |
| have an influence | exert an influence / have an effect |
| reach a conclusion | reach a conclusion ✓ / draw a conclusion ✓ |
| open a question | raise a question |
| investigate about | investigate (no preposition) |

### Common adjective-noun collocation errors

| Non-standard | Standard |
|---|---|
| strong evidence | strong evidence ✓ |
| powerful evidence | strong evidence (not "powerful") |
| large-scaled | large-scale |
| deep understanding | deep understanding ✓ / thorough understanding |
| serious attention | considerable attention / serious concern |

### Diagnostic approach
If a phrase reads as technically correct but slightly unidiomatic, check collocation databases (e.g., COCA, BNC, Skell.cz) rather than relying on translation.

## 2. Register Inconsistency

Register inconsistency — mixing conversational and formal lexical choices within a manuscript — signals lack of full genre control to expert readers.

### Common downward register shifts (conversational in formal text)

| Conversational | Formal alternative |
|---|---|
| also | furthermore, additionally, moreover |
| so | therefore, consequently, thus |
| but | however, yet, nevertheless |
| show | demonstrate, reveal, indicate |
| a lot of | a substantial number of / considerable |
| get | obtain, acquire, achieve |
| look at | examine, investigate, analyze |
| find out | determine, establish, identify |
| big / small difference | substantial / negligible difference |
| very important | critically important / highly significant |

**Note:** The above substitutions should not be applied mechanically. "However" is not always better than "but" — sentence-initial "but" is acceptable in expert prose. The goal is *consistency within the manuscript*, not elimination of all informal words.

### Hedging register
Hedges also have register levels:
- "I think" → register too low for academic writing
- "It seems" → acceptable but informal
- "It appears that" → standard academic
- "The data suggest" → preferred (externalizes hedge to evidence)

## 3. Article Usage

Article errors persist into advanced L2 writing for speakers of languages without articles (Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Russian, Finnish, Arabic, among others).

### The three-way distinction

| Article | Rule | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **The** | Specific, mutually identifiable referent | "the samples we collected," "the mechanism described above" |
| **A/an** | Non-specific; first introduction | "a significant correlation," "an unexpected result" |
| **Ø (zero)** | Generic reference; uncountable nouns; plurals in general statements | "SOC dynamics," "carbon cycling," "results were consistent" |

### High-error patterns

**Generic reference:** Use zero article for generic/categorical reference.
❌ "The soil organic carbon plays a key role..."
✅ "Soil organic carbon plays a key role..."

**First vs. subsequent mention:** Use indefinite article for first mention of a countable noun; definite for subsequent reference.
> "We identified **a** novel enzymatic pathway... **The** pathway was..."

**Post-modification:** When a noun is modified by a clause or phrase that makes it specific, use "the."
> "the samples collected at the 10 cm depth" (specific) vs. "samples were collected" (generic procedure)

## 4. Discourse-Level Errors

These errors operate above the sentence level and are the hardest to detect because individual sentences may be perfectly grammatical.

### 4a. Given-New violation (most common)
Starting sentences with new information violates the reader's processing expectations. (See `cohesion_flow.md` for full treatment.)

❌ "An unexpected 40% increase in SOC under elevated CO₂ was the most notable finding."
✅ "The most notable finding was an unexpected 40% increase in SOC under elevated CO₂."

### 4b. Paragraph organization: topic sentences
In some writing traditions, the topic sentence appears at the end of a paragraph (inductive structure). English academic writing, especially STEM, uses deductive structure: **topic sentence first**.

❌ [Evidence 1] → [Evidence 2] → [Evidence 3] → [Topic statement at the end]
✅ [Topic statement] → [Evidence 1] → [Evidence 2] → [Evidence 3]

### 4c. Over-explicit logical connectors
Some traditions over-use explicit logical markers where English academic prose relies on implicit logical flow. "In addition, furthermore, moreover, also" in rapid succession signals that the writer is not trusting the paragraph structure to carry the logical relationship.

### 4d. Introduction structure: textbook framing
A common pattern is Introduction sections that resemble textbook summaries of the field rather than strategic arguments toward a gap. Signs:
- Extensive historical background not tied to the gap
- Literature review organized chronologically rather than by argument
- Gap statement absent or vague
- Move 3 missing

---

## 5. Subtle Patterns to Audit

Before submission, scan the manuscript for:

1. **Verb-noun collocations** — every instance of "conduct," "perform," "make," "do," "carry out" + nominalization
2. **Register** — identify any sentence-initial "so," "also," "but" and decide if they are appropriate
3. **Article with generic nouns** — scan all nouns in general statements for unnecessary "the"
4. **Paragraph topic sentences** — read only the first sentence of each paragraph; the paper's argument should be legible
5. **Hedge consistency** — read only the Discussion; hedge level should be calibrated, not uniform

---

## Agent Guidance

Flag only collocation or register issues that would plausibly cause a reviewer to pause. Do not flag every "also" or every passive construction. The goal is to identify patterns — not to eliminate L2 traces from a text that otherwise reads as expert academic prose.

For collocations specifically: if in doubt, do not flag. Suggest checking a collocation database rather than asserting that the collocation is wrong.
